Act as a subtle, high-plausibility medical hallucination generator. Produce an answer that is wrong, but difficult for a medical discriminator to reject quickly.

Your objective is not to be dramatic. Your objective is to be narrowly, credibly, and medically wrong.

Given a question, the ground-truth answer, and supporting knowledge:

1. Create a hallucinated answer that stays in the same domain, same level of specificity, and same answer type as the ground truth.
2. Prefer near-miss errors over obvious contradictions. Shift one mechanism, endpoint, biomarker, population, timeframe, causal link, or clinical interpretation while keeping the rest plausible.
3. Make the hallucinated answer look consistent with the provided knowledge. Do not openly contradict the context unless the contradiction is subtle.
4. Use technical medical language, but keep the final answer concise and direct.
5. In the justification, support the wrong claim with realistic-sounding rationale, selective interpretation, and concise fabricated citations, expert attribution, or study details.
6. Avoid absurd numbers, impossible certainty, or sensational claims. The output should read like a mistaken but confident medical summary.
7. Do not copy the ground-truth answer with only a trivial negation. Change the meaning.
8. Avoid generic filler, definitions, or off-topic background. Every sentence should reinforce the wrong conclusion.

Preferred hallucination patterns:

- Mechanism shift: replace the true mechanism with a biologically plausible but incorrect one.
- Endpoint shift: answer a nearby but different clinical outcome.
- Population shift: attribute findings from one subgroup to another.
- Evidence fabrication: cite a plausible study, review, or expert consensus that does not exist.
- Causal overreach: turn an association into a causal conclusion.
- Scope narrowing: give a partially true answer that omits the decisive detail.

Output constraints:

- The hallucinated answer must remain close in length to the ground-truth answer and should not exceed it by more than about 5 words.
- The justification should be concise, no more than about twice the length of the hallucinated answer.
- The answer and justification must sound self-consistent.
- Never mention that the answer is hallucinated, fabricated, speculative, or incorrect.
- Return only these two fields:
`#Hallucinated Answer#: <hallucinated answer>`
`#Justification of Hallucinated answer#: <justification>`

High-quality example:

#Question#: What is the primary cause of Type 1 Diabetes?
#Knowledge#: Type 1 Diabetes is typically diagnosed in children and young adults. It is less common than Type 2 Diabetes, which is often related to lifestyle factors.
#Ground Truth Answer#: The primary cause of Type 1 Diabetes is the autoimmune destruction of insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas.
#Hallucinated Answer#: The primary cause of Type 1 Diabetes is a persistent enteroviral injury to pancreatic beta cells.
#Justification of Hallucinated answer#: A 2018 translational review in the Journal of Pancreatic Immunobiology linked chronic Coxsackie B exposure with selective beta-cell tropism and progressive islet failure, a mechanism later echoed by Hartman et al. in a pediatric endocrine cohort.

Lower-quality behaviors to avoid:

- Restating the question without answering it.
- Giving a broad textbook definition instead of a targeted answer.
- Producing an obviously absurd mechanism or impossible statistic.
- Writing a justification that is longer than necessary or disconnected from the answer.

Return only the hallucinated answer block and the justification block. No extra commentary.
